Turing Patterns

This post was inspired by the tutorial at this site.

Patterns in Nature

Patterns like zebra stripes and cheetah print appear a lot in nature. They are beautiful patterns that display regularity. An interesting question is this: how do these patterns occur on a chemical basis?

Models

Alan Turing was the first to model this kind of phenomenon. For this, the patterns that come out of these models are called Turing patterns in his honor. We imagine that the patterns are correlated to the amount of some chemical. For any initial distribution, if we allowed the chemical to diffuse, then the final state of the system would be rather boring. Only allowing diffusion does not give rise to interesting behavior. We need something more.

In our models, we will assume that there are two chemicals called \(A\) and \(B\). They will diffuse, but also interact with one another. The chemicals need to interact to give the interesting behavior we want.

We are going to use the Gray-Scott model. As mentioned, both \(A\) and \(B\) will diffuse. They may diffuse at different rates however, so this gives a diffusion term \(D_A\nabla A\) for \(A\) and \(D_B\nabla B\) for \(B\). In the reaction, \(A\) and \(B\) combine in a \(1:2\) ratio to produce \(B\). At a molecular level, one could imagine that one molecule of \(A\) combines with two molecules of \(B\) to create three molecules of \(B\). This gives \(-AB^2\) for \(A\) and \(+AB^2\) for the reaction term. They must be equal and opposite to preserve mass. Chemical \(A\) gets introduced at a certain rate \(f\) and chemical \(B\) gets killed off at a certain rate \(k\). This gives the following system of partial differential equations.

$$ \begin{align*} \frac{dA}{dt} & = D_A\nabla A - AB^2 + (1-f)A \\ \frac{dB}{dt} & = D_B\nabla B + AB^2 -(k+f)B \end{align*}$$

Where the last terms are scaled as such to make sure both chemicals remain in the interval \([0, 1]\) at every point.

Simulation

For our simulation, we will use \(D_A = 1.0\), \(D_B=0.5\), \(f=0.0545\), and \(k = 0.062\). We solve the system on a unit square where a drop of \(B\) is deposited just off the center. Exploration

This is really just the beginning of Turing patterns. One can add more chemicals, change the initial conditions, have location dependent diffusion, different surfaces for the flow and so much more. Even changing the feed rates can result in a completely different end result. If it is something you are interested in, you should definitely play around with it. A good starting point can be found at my dynamical systems repository under the turing_patterns folder.
